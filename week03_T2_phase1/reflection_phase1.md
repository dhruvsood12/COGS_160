# Phase 1 — Reflection: Boolean Strings vs. Natural-Language Search

**Student:** Dhruv Sood · **COGS 160 Track 2 · Week 3** · ~520 words

Three days of running the same research front through PubMed Boolean strings and through the natural-language interfaces (Elicit, Google AI Scholar, Consensus, Scholar AI) sharpened a distinction the assignment text only states abstractly: Boolean and natural-language search are not two ways of asking the same question. They are two ways of asking *different* questions, and which one is appropriate depends entirely on what you already know.

A Boolean query is a *commitment to a vocabulary*. When I wrote `("Strategic Management Simulation" OR SMS) AND (CO2 OR "carbon dioxide")` I was claiming, in advance, that the literature I want already uses those exact strings. The query yielded 41 hits but the SMS abbreviation matched mostly Short Message Service and surgical mask studies — useless noise. The fix was not to change the question, but to drop a token from the vocabulary commitment. Boolean search punishes you whenever the literature has converged on a phrasing you don't yet know. The flip side is precision: my best query (Q03, `(CO2 OR "carbon dioxide") AND (cognitive OR cognition) AND ppm`) returned 32 hits with five clear includes — every single hit was a paper that explicitly reported a CO₂ concentration in ppm and tied it to a cognitive outcome. No natural-language tool I tried matched that hit-rate-per-token-of-effort.

Natural-language search is the inverse. When I drafted the Elicit prompt *"What is the lowest indoor CO₂ concentration at which adult decision-making performance has been shown to decline?"* I was *not* committing to vocabulary; I was offloading the vocabulary problem onto the retrieval model. The cost is opacity — I cannot read the query plan, cannot inspect why a paper was retrieved, cannot enumerate the false negatives. The benefit is that the model can map "decision-making" to SMS, to executive-function batteries, to driving-error rates, to chess-move quality, all in one shot. The Boolean query that would replicate Elicit's recall here would be unreadable: a six-line OR chain across instrument names, plus all the synonyms for "decline" and "concentration."

The two modes are also asymmetric on falsifiability. PubMed gave me a hit count I could verify and a JSON of PMIDs I could re-fetch tomorrow with the same result. Elicit and Consensus return ranked lists shaped by an opaque model. If I cite a Consensus verdict in the Phase 5 report I cannot reproduce that verdict deterministically — which is fine for orientation but bad for any claim I want to defend.

What this implies for Track 2's pipeline: Boolean search is the right tool for the **canon** (anchor authors, instrument names, well-defined dose-response thresholds — the Satish/Allen/MacNaughton stream) and natural-language search is the right tool for the **frontier** (gaps, contested claims, cross-domain analogies — the heat-stress × risk-taking literature, the chess × air-pollution finding that PubMed could not surface because the paper was an IZA discussion paper). The Question Maker for Phase 2 should not pick a winner; it should explicitly route different question shapes to different tools, and the diary should record both the routing decision and its hit-rate so we can revise the routing for Phase 4.
